# Part 3: Regression-Based Business Insights & Model Interpretation

**Dataset:** business_regression_data.xlsx | 320 observations × 14 columns

---

## Business Problem Summary

A retail chain leadership team wants to understand what factors are driving monthly sales performance across stores. They are evaluating business actions such as increasing marketing spend, improving inventory availability, changing discounting strategy, reallocating staff, and prioritising certain store types or regions. Regression analysis was used to identify which factors are most strongly associated with monthly sales and to provide an evidence-based recommendation.

## Regression Approach

Four models were estimated:

| # | Model | Type | Predictors |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Simple – footfall | Simple Linear Regression | footfall |
| 2 | Simple – marketing_spend | Simple Linear Regression | marketing_spend |
| 3 | Simple – staff_count | Simple Linear Regression | staff_count |
| 4 | **Multiple Regression (FINAL)** | Multiple Linear Regression | footfall, marketing_spend, inventory_availability_pct, staff_count + 6 dummy variables |

All models used Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) estimation. Statistical significance was assessed at the 5% level (p < 0.05).

---

## Dummy Variable Approach

Two categorical variables were converted into dummy variables:

### Region (4 categories → 3 dummies)
- **Reference category: East** (largest group, 104 observations)
- Dummies created: `region_North`, `region_South`, `region_West`
- Interpretation: Each dummy coefficient shows the sales difference vs East stores, all else equal

### Store Type (4 categories → 3 dummies)
- **Reference category: Airport** (highest-performing format)
- Dummies created: `store_High Street`, `store_Mall`, `store_Residential`
- Interpretation: Each dummy coefficient shows the sales difference vs Airport stores, all else equal

The dummy variable trap was avoided by dropping one category per variable (the reference). Using all 4 categories simultaneously would create perfect collinearity and make OLS estimation impossible.

## Final Model Selected

**Model 4 — Multiple Regression** is the selected final model.

```
monthly_sales = 126,369.77
  + 28.8291  × footfall
  + 1.1736   × marketing_spend
  + 3,003.33 × inventory_availability_pct
  + 2,790.49 × staff_count
  + 7,601.23 × region_North
  + 20,900.94 × region_South
  + 18,892.97 × region_West
  − 22,531.68 × store_High Street
  − 10,340.14 × store_Mall
  − 39,934.03 × store_Residential
```

**R-Squared: 0.8296 | Adjusted R-Squared: 0.8240 | F-statistic: 150.4 (p < 2.29e-112)**

---

## Business Recommendation

Based on regression evidence from Model 4:

1. **Prioritise footfall-driving initiatives** — footfall is by far the most impactful lever (R²=73.6% in isolation). Every additional monthly visitor contributes ~₹29 in sales.

2. **Improve inventory availability to 90%+** — each 1% improvement adds ~₹3,003 in monthly sales. High-impact, directly controllable operational improvement.

3. **Focus new store investments on Airport and high-performing Mall locations** — Residential stores generate ~₹39,934 less per month than Airport stores; High Street stores ~₹22,532 less. Format selection is a long-term strategic decision with significant financial consequences.

4. **Prioritise South and West regions** — both carry statistically significant sales premiums over East (+₹20,901 and +₹18,893 respectively). New openings and resource uplift should weight these regions.

5. **Audit high-residual stores** — five stores outperformed predictions by ₹97K–₹1.13L (learning opportunities); five stores underperformed by ₹97K–₹1.61L (intervention targets).

6. **Do not over-invest based on discounting** — `avg_discount_pct` showed no positive relationship with sales in this data.

## Assumptions and Limitations

| Assumption / Limitation | Detail |
|---|---|
| OLS linearity assumption | Assumed linear relationship between predictors and monthly_sales |
| No autocorrelation | Durbin-Watson = 2.021; no significant autocorrelation detected |
| Homoscedasticity | Assumed; not formally tested with White test |
| No perfect multicollinearity | Dummy trap avoided; condition number 1.06e+06 indicates some collinearity risk |
| Short time window | Only 4 months of data (Jan–Apr 2025); seasonal patterns not captured |
| Missing value imputation | 14 records imputed with medians; may introduce small bias |
| Observational data | Regression shows association, not causation |
| Unexplained variance | 17% of monthly sales variation remains unexplained |
